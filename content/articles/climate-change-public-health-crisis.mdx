For most of the last two decades, climate change has been framed publicly as an environmental issue: a matter of emissions targets, energy policy, and biodiversity loss. That framing is not wrong, but it is incomplete in a way that has real consequences. It lets health systems — which plan for hospital capacity, disease surveillance, and workforce needs — treat climate change as an external, distant risk rather than a driver of the demand they are already managing.

The evidence no longer supports that separation.

## What the evidence actually shows

Three mechanisms connect a warming climate to health outcomes with reasonable directness, each backed by a substantial and growing evidence base.

**Direct heat exposure.** Extreme heat is already the deadliest weather-related hazard in Europe. Heat stress contributes to cardiovascular strain, kidney injury, and worsens outcomes for people with existing chronic conditions, with older adults and outdoor workers at disproportionate risk.

**Shifting disease geography.** Warmer temperatures and changing rainfall patterns are expanding the range of vector-borne diseases. Mosquito species capable of transmitting dengue and chikungunya have established populations further north in Europe than they held a generation ago.

**Compounding food and water insecurity.** Drought and extreme weather affect crop yields and water quality, with downstream effects on nutrition and infectious disease risk that tend to fall hardest on lower-income populations, both globally and within wealthy countries.

<Callout type="evidence" title="Scientific Evidence">
The Lancet Countdown's 2024 report estimated that heat-related mortality among people over 65 has risen significantly over the past two decades across the populations it tracks, a trend attributed in large part to warming rather than demographic change alone.
</Callout>

None of these mechanisms are speculative. What is less settled is the pace and local distribution of impact — which is precisely the kind of uncertainty health systems are usually asked to plan around anyway.

## Why the "environmental issue" framing has held on

There are structural reasons the health dimension of climate change has been slow to enter mainstream health policy, beyond simple neglect.

1. **Budget silos.** Environment ministries and health ministries in most countries operate with separate mandates, separate budgets, and limited formal mechanisms for joint planning.
2. **Attribution difficulty.** A heatwave death is usually recorded as a cardiac or respiratory event, not a "climate" event, which makes the health burden of climate change harder to see in routine statistics.
3. **Planning horizons.** Health systems are typically funded and evaluated on short political cycles, while climate impacts compound over decades — a mismatch that discourages long-term adaptation investment.
4. **Competing acute priorities.** When a health system is already under strain from workforce shortages or backlogs, a slow-moving risk multiplier is easy to deprioritise relative to immediate pressures.

<Callout type="analysis" title="Analysis">
These are not failures of understanding so much as failures of institutional design. The evidence base for treating climate as a health issue has been strong for over a decade; what has lagged is the administrative machinery to act on it across sectors that were never built to talk to each other.
</Callout>

## What "treating it as a public health crisis" would actually mean

Framing matters because it changes who is responsible, what gets measured, and where funding flows. Concretely, treating climate change as a public health crisis would involve:

- Integrating heat-health early warning systems into routine public health infrastructure, not just emergency response
- Building climate projections into long-term hospital capacity and workforce planning
- Expanding surveillance for climate-sensitive infectious diseases before they become established
- Addressing the disproportionate exposure of low-income and elderly populations through housing and urban planning policy, not health policy alone
- Training healthcare professionals to recognise and communicate climate-related health risks to patients

<KeyTakeaways>
- Heat, shifting disease patterns, and food/water insecurity are the three best-evidenced pathways linking climate change to health outcomes.
- Health systems have been slow to adapt largely due to budget silos and short planning horizons, not a lack of evidence.
- Reframing climate change as a health issue would shift funding, surveillance, and planning responsibility toward institutions better equipped to act on it.
</KeyTakeaways>

<PullQuote cite="The Lancet Countdown, 2024">
Every additional fraction of a degree of warming will steadily increase the impacts on health and amplify existing health inequities.
</PullQuote>

## What remains genuinely uncertain

It's worth being precise about the limits of current evidence, both because overstating certainty undermines credibility and because the genuine uncertainties matter for policy design.

<Callout type="uncertainty" title="What Remains Uncertain">
Local-level projections — how a specific city's heat-health burden will evolve over the next 20 years, for instance — carry wider error margins than global trend estimates. Adaptation investment decisions often have to be made under that uncertainty rather than waiting for it to resolve, which is itself a policy challenge worth acknowledging openly.
</Callout>

There is also legitimate debate about sequencing: whether resource-constrained health systems should prioritise climate adaptation now, or continue focusing on more immediate access and workforce gaps first. Reasonable people, including public health professionals, disagree on this trade-off, and the answer likely varies by country and system capacity.

## My view

I think the "environmental issue" framing has outlived its usefulness. It made sense when the connection to health was still being established; it doesn't reflect where the evidence stands now. The practical cost of the old framing isn't abstract — it shows up as hospitals caught underprepared for heatwaves that were, in a meteorological sense, entirely predictable.

<Callout type="opinion" title="Author's Opinion">
I don't think this requires health systems to take over climate policy. It requires them to stop treating climate risk as external to their planning — the way they already plan for seasonal flu or an aging population. The infrastructure for that kind of anticipatory planning already exists in public health; it's a matter of pointing it at a risk that has been sitting in plain sight.
</Callout>

None of this is a substitute for a doctor's advice about your own health, particularly if you are in a group — older adults, people with chronic cardiovascular or respiratory conditions, outdoor workers — at heightened risk during extreme heat events. It is, however, a case for why the systems meant to protect population health need to start planning for a climate that has already changed.

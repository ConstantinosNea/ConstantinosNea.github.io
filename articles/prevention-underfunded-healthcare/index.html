Why Prevention Remains Underfunded in Modern Healthcare

Health systems say prevention is a priority. Budgets tell a different story.

By Elena MichaelidesFebruary 27, 20263 min read
#prevention#health-inequalities#healthcare-operations#european-health-policy

Share this article

Across almost every health system in Europe, preventive care receives a strikingly small share of total health spending — typically in the low single digits as a percentage of total health expenditure, according to OECD data — while the vast majority goes to treating illness after it develops. This is not a controversial observation; health economists and policymakers acknowledge it openly. What's more interesting is why it persists despite decades of evidence that prevention offers strong long-term value.

This isn't a knowledge problem

It would be easy to assume policymakers simply don't know the evidence for prevention. That's not the honest explanation. The case for prevention — vaccination, screening programmes, tobacco and alcohol policy, occupational health — is well established and rarely disputed in health policy circles. The gap between evidence and funding is structural.

  1. Budget cycles are short; prevention's payoff is long. A smoking cessation programme funded today may not show up as reduced treatment costs for fifteen or twenty years — well past the term of the government that funded it.
  2. Treatment has an identifiable patient; prevention has a statistic. Money spent treating a specific, visible patient generates political and emotional legibility that money spent lowering a population-level risk rate does not.
  3. Acute care is where the political pressure concentrates. Waiting lists and emergency department crowding generate headlines and public pressure in a way that a gradually improving population health trend does not.
  4. Reimbursement systems are built around treatment. Most health system financing, from hospital payment models to specialist reimbursement, is structured around treating episodes of illness, not around preventing them.
Analysis

None of these four factors is a moral failing on the part of health administrators. They are rational responses to the incentive structures those administrators operate within. Which is precisely why "raising awareness" of prevention's value tends to have limited effect — the constraint isn't awareness, it's institutional design.

Where this shows up most starkly: health inequalities

Important Caveat

Underfunded prevention doesn't affect all populations equally. Preventive services — screening uptake, occupational health protections, health literacy programmes — tend to reach higher-income, more health-literate populations more effectively than lower-income groups, even when nominally universal. Underfunding prevention as a category, therefore, tends to widen existing health inequalities rather than simply lowering average outcomes uniformly.

What would actually shift the balance

I don't think moral appeals to "value prevention more" accomplish much on their own. What has worked, in the cases where prevention funding has meaningfully increased, tends to involve changing the structural incentives directly:

  • Ring-fenced, multi-year prevention budgets that survive single political cycles
  • Outcome-linked financing that rewards reduced disease incidence, not just treatment volume
  • Cross-government accountability (housing, education, environment) for population health outcomes, not health ministries alone
  • Independent, longer-horizon institutions — modelled on the UK's now-diminished Public Health England or similar bodies — insulated somewhat from annual budget pressure
Key Takeaways
  • Preventive care receives a small share of total health spending across OECD countries, despite strong long-term evidence for its value.
  • The gap is structural — short budget cycles, weaker political visibility, and treatment-oriented reimbursement systems — not a lack of evidence.
  • Underfunded prevention tends to widen health inequalities, since preventive services usually reach advantaged populations more effectively.
  • Meaningful change likely requires restructuring incentives (ring-fenced budgets, outcome-linked financing) rather than raising awareness alone.
Author's Opinion

This is where I'll be direct: I think health systems that describe prevention as a "priority" while allocating it a low single-digit share of spending are, in effect, making a different priority statement through their budgets than through their rhetoric. That's not unique to healthcare — most institutions reveal priorities through resource allocation more honestly than through mission statements — but the cost of the gap, measured in preventable disease burden, is unusually high in this sector.

This is an opinion piece grounded in publicly available spending data; reasonable analysts disagree on the right balance between prevention and treatment investment, particularly in systems facing acute capacity crises where the trade-off is genuinely difficult.
